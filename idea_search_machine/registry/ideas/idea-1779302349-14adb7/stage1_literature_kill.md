## Q1

**Is this relativizing?**

Not killed by Baker-Gill-Solovay 1975 (https://epubs.siam.org/doi/10.1137/0204037), because the proposed argument routes through 2D SFT entropy, a combinatorial/dynamical invariant of explicit local rules. Such invariants are not preserved under oracle access — the circuit-to-SFT encoding fundamentally uses the gate structure, not query behavior. So in principle the argument is non-relativizing. **However**, this only means relativization is not the killer; the question of whether the *content* survives is what Q2–Q8 address.

## Q2

**Is this natural?**

Conditional kill: Razborov-Rudich, *Natural Proofs*, JCSS 55 (1997), 24–35 (https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022000097914948), applies under the assumption that the entropy-based invariant is (a) constructive (computable in time $2^{O(n)}$ on truth tables) and (b) large (holds for a noticeable fraction of functions). The proposal *claims* evasion because entropy is $\Pi_2$-complete (Hochman–Meyerovitch), hence not constructive. **But** the load-bearing step requires that for a *correct* polynomial circuit, $h(X_n)$ becomes polynomial-time approximable — which is precisely a constructive property of the circuit's truth-table behavior on the relevant inputs. Once you have a constructive approximation procedure that distinguishes "decides $L$" from "doesn't," you have a natural property in disguise. The author has not exhibited the asymmetry needed to escape this. This is a serious conditional kill.

## Q3

**Is this algebrizing?**

Not killed by Aaronson–Wigderson, *Algebrization*, TOCT 1 (2009) (https://www.scottaaronson.com/papers/alg.pdf), because SFT entropy arguments do not obviously lift to low-degree extensions; the local-rule structure has no natural arithmetization preserving entropy. So algebrization is not the killer.

## Q4

**Is this a known hardness-magnification dead end?**

Killed (partial) by Chen, Hirahara, Oliveira, Pich, Rajgopal, Santhanam, *Beyond Natural Proofs: Hardness Magnification and Locality*, JACM 69 (2022) (https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3538391, also https://eccc.weizmann.ac.il/report/2019/164/), because the proposed argument is a **local reduction** in disguise: the Aubrun–Sablik simulation theorem produces SFTs whose local rules have window size polynomial in $s(n)$, i.e., the reduction is local in exactly the sense the locality barrier formalizes. Chen et al. show that local-reduction-based lower bounds cannot cross the magnification threshold without independently solving the underlying lower bound. The proposal's "dynamical lower bound independent of circuit model" is the kind of clean reduction that the locality barrier explicitly rules out as a route to $P \ne NP$. This is a hard kill on the *strategy*.

## Q5

**Is this equivalent to a known open lower bound?**

Conditional kill: deciding bounded tiling languages with NP-hardness (Robinson; Berger) is well-studied, and lower bounds on these are equivalent to general circuit lower bounds for NP (since they're NP-complete under standard reductions). So "superpolynomial circuit lower bound for tiling-NP-complete $L$" *is* $P \ne NP$, not a stepping stone. The proposal does not exhibit any restricted version of $L$ that would constitute a weaker, achievable target. See Emde Boas, *The Convenience of Tilings*, in *Complexity, Logic, and Recursion Theory* (1997) (https://staff.fnwi.uva.nl/p.vanemdeboas/tilings.pdf).

## Q6

**Is this already proved impossible?**

Not found after search. (Searched: "circuit lower bound SFT entropy", "Hochman Meyerovitch complexity", "topological entropy P vs NP", "subshift circuit complexity barrier".) No direct refutation of the *exact* scheme, but see Q4 for a barrier that the scheme falls under.

## Q7

**Is this already known to be too weak?**

Not directly applicable — the proposal targets superpolynomial bounds for an NP-complete language, which is above magnification threshold (it *would* prove $P \ne NP$ if it worked). So weakness is not the issue; rather, strength + locality is the issue (Q4).

## Q8

**Is there a paper that directly says "this type of route does not work"?**

Killed by Chen–Jin–Williams, *Sharp threshold results for computational complexity*, STOC 2020 (https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3357713.3384283), and reinforced by the OPS19 threshold-gap analysis (Oliveira–Pich–Santhanam, CCC 2019, https://drops.dagstuhl.de/opus/volltexte/2019/10849/): any approach that constructs a uniform local invariant (entropy of an SFT generated by the circuit) and tries to derive a polynomial-rate-computability contradiction is exactly the magnification-style argument these papers show *cannot* be pushed past known lower bounds without a non-local ingredient. The proposal supplies no non-local ingredient — Hochman–Meyerovitch and Gangloff–Sablik are theorems about local rules, hence local.

Also relevant: Destainville–Mosseri–Bailly type results, and more directly, **Hochman, *On the dynamics and recursive properties of multidimensional symbolic systems*, Inventiones 176 (2009)** (https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00222-008-0161-7), shows that the recursion-theoretic content of SFT entropy is rigidly $\Pi_2$ and resists quantitative refinement of the form Gangloff–Sablik would need to push to polynomial rates per input length — the Gangloff–Sablik bounds are asymptotic in the SFT, not in any uniformity parameter usable for circuit families. The quantitative bridge the proposal needs ($2^{\Omega(n^\delta)}$ approximation time governed by $\alpha_n$) is not in Gangloff–Sablik; it would have to be proved separately, and there is no reason to expect it.

## Final verdict

The proposal is killed primarily by the **locality barrier (Chen et al. JACM 2022)**: the circuit-to-SFT encoding via Aubrun–Sablik is a paradigmatically local reduction, and the entropy invariant is computed from local rules. The natural-proofs evasion claim is unsubstantiated (Q2). The quantitative bridge from Gangloff–Sablik is misread — those bounds do not give per-input-length polynomial-rate obstructions of the kind needed. Independently, the strategy proves $P \ne NP$ directly without a magnification stepping stone, but cannot because of locality.
